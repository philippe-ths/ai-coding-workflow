# Evaluation Methodology

Primary-source notes on statistical design, LLM-as-judge reliability, and evaluation pitfalls for AI agent workflow comparisons.
See `design/research/README.md` for the citation convention.

## Zheng et al. — LLM-as-judge position, verbosity, and self-enhancement biases {#zheng-llm-judge-biases}

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05685
Extracted: 2026-04-23
Finding: The NeurIPS 2023 paper "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena" (Zheng et al.) empirically examines three LLM-judge biases. Position bias: LLM judges change their verdict depending on which candidate answer is presented first or second; the authors report that GPT-4's judgment consistency under answer-order swaps is roughly 65.0% in a zero-shot setting, rising to about 77.5% with a few-shot judge. Verbosity bias: in a "repetitive list" attack designed to pad answers without adding information, both GPT-3.5 and Claude-v1 show a systematic preference for the longer, repetitive answer over an equivalent concise one. Self-enhancement bias: LLM judges tend to prefer answers generated by themselves over answers from other models when human preferences are used as ground truth. The paper also reports that GPT-4-as-judge reaches above 80% agreement with human preferences, comparable to inter-human agreement, which is the basis for using LLM-as-judge at all. Recommended mitigation for position bias is to run the judge twice with swapped positions and only declare a winner when both orderings agree; otherwise call it a tie.

## Panickssery, Bowman, Feng — self-recognition drives self-preference in LLM evaluators {#panickssery-self-preference}

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.13076
Extracted: 2026-04-23
Finding: "LLM Evaluators Recognize and Favor Their Own Generations" (Panickssery et al., 2024) isolates self-preference bias — where an LLM judge scores its own outputs higher than others' while human annotators rate them as equal quality — and links it causally to self-recognition. Out of the box, GPT-4 and Llama 2 can distinguish their own generations from other LLMs' and humans' above chance. By fine-tuning the models to vary self-recognition strength, the authors find a linear relationship between self-recognition accuracy and self-preference magnitude; controlled experiments rule out simple confounders. The practical consequence for evaluation methodology is that using a model as judge of its own outputs (or of pipelines that use the same model) introduces a bias that scales with how recognizable those outputs are to the judge, and cannot be eliminated by prompt engineering alone.

## Shankar et al. — criteria drift and the fragility of LLM-graded evaluation {#shankar-criteria-drift}

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.12272
Extracted: 2026-04-23
Finding: "Who Validates the Validators?" (Shankar, Zamfirescu-Pereira, Hartmann, Parameswaran, Arawjo; UIST 2024) reports a qualitative study of users building LLM-as-judge evaluators. The central empirical finding is a phenomenon the authors name "criteria drift": participants needed to grade concrete outputs in order to define their evaluation criteria, and continued to refine criteria as they graded more outputs — sometimes revising earlier grades. Some criteria were dependent on the specific LLM outputs observed rather than definable a priori. The authors frame this as a challenge for any approach that assumes evaluation criteria can be fixed in advance of looking at model outputs, and the paper argues that LLM-generated evaluators inherit the problems of the underlying LLM and therefore require human validation rather than being trusted as standalone evidence.

## Dietterich — McNemar's test as the recommended paired comparison for classifiers {#dietterich-mcnemar-paired-test}

Source: https://direct.mit.edu/neco/article-abstract/10/7/1895/6224/Approximate-Statistical-Tests-for-Comparing
Extracted: 2026-04-23
Finding: Dietterich's 1998 Neural Computation paper "Approximate Statistical Tests for Comparing Supervised Classification Learning Algorithms" reviews five tests for deciding whether one learning algorithm outperforms another on a task and measures their Type I error rates experimentally. Two common tests — a difference-of-proportions test and a paired t-test over random train/test splits — are shown to have high Type I error and are recommended against. McNemar's test, applied to the 2x2 contingency table of paired correct/incorrect outcomes on a shared test set, has acceptable Type I error. The explicit recommendation is: when algorithms can only be run once (a single evaluation on a fixed test set), McNemar's test is the only one of the five with acceptable Type I error; when algorithms can be run ten times, the 5x2cv test is preferred. The test is paired by construction — it uses only the off-diagonal cells of the contingency table, i.e. the items on which the two systems disagree — which is why it applies cleanly to comparing two workflow versions on the same task set with pass/fail outcomes.

## Bonferroni and FDR corrections for multiple comparisons {#multiple-comparisons-correction}

Source: https://physiology.med.cornell.edu/people/banfelder/qbio/resources_2008/1.5_Bonferroni_FDR.pdf
Extracted: 2026-04-23
Finding: The familywise error rate (FWER) is the probability of at least one false positive across a family of tests. If each of n independent tests uses significance threshold alpha, the probability of at least one false positive under the null grows roughly as 1-(1-alpha)^n; at alpha=0.05 and 20 independent tests the expected rate of spurious "significant" findings is already about 64%. The Bonferroni correction controls FWER by testing each hypothesis at alpha/n, at the cost of reduced statistical power and conservatism that worsens as n grows. The Benjamini–Hochberg procedure instead controls the false discovery rate (FDR) — the expected fraction of false positives among rejected hypotheses — and is generally preferred when n is larger than a handful because it retains more power. The methodological implication for workflow comparisons is that running many pairwise statistical tests (across metrics, prompt clusters, or versions) without any correction inflates false positives roughly linearly in the number of tests.

## Liu et al. — G-Eval notes judge bias toward LLM-generated text {#g-eval-llm-bias}

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.16634
Extracted: 2026-04-23
Finding: "G-Eval: NLG Evaluation using GPT-4 with Better Human Alignment" (Liu, Iter, Xu, Wang, Xu, Zhu; EMNLP 2023) proposes a chain-of-thought plus form-filling framework for using GPT-4 to score NLG outputs. On summarization, G-Eval with GPT-4 achieves a Spearman correlation of 0.514 with human judgments, substantially above prior automatic metrics. Beyond the headline result, the paper explicitly notes that LLM-based evaluators show a bias toward LLM-generated text over human-written reference text — that is, the judge systematically prefers the style of the family of model it is drawn from. This is independent evidence, from a different task setup than MT-Bench, that LLM-as-judge scores are not an unbiased proxy for quality and should be treated as a hypothesis-generation signal rather than a conclusion when the scored outputs also come from LLMs.
